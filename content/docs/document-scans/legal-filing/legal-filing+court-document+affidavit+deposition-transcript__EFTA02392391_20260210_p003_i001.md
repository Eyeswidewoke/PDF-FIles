Your chapter reads well and is certainly more than fair about me, given the general unfairness I've experienced 
from militant atheists and hard-boiled skeptics. Thanks for being so open-minded. 
My only comments have to do with making a difficult subject more accessible to your readers. 
1. I'd slow down at the passage about "redness" and spell out that the brain is dark but we see light' the brain is 
silent, but we hear' the brain contains no pictures, but we see full-blown three-dimensional images. Then open up how a 
neuron works, firing the same chemical signals as any other cell with small modifications. So how does a brain cell, born 
of the same strand of DNA as a stomach o liver cell, think? It seems to me that a brain cell cannot think. 
2. On a basic chemical level, the sugar in a sugar cube or the glucose in the bloodstream has no special 
properties; the molecules do not participate in mental activity until they enter the brain. You might want to point out 
this mystery and also note that material monists must show the exact point when molecules exhibit mind-like behavior. 
3. Freeman Dyson, among other physicists, cites the mind-like behavior of subatomic particles as they "choose" 
and "decide" what state in the wave-particle duality to exist in. Here's a nice quote from Dyson: 
"It would not be surprising that the origin and destiny of the 
energy in the universe cannot be completely understood in isolation from the 
phenomena of life and consciousness--It is conceivable---that life may have a 
larger role to play than we have imagined." 
"Life may have succeeded against all odds in molding a universe to its purposes. And the design of the inanimate 
universe may not be as detached from the potentialities of life and intelligence 
as scientists of the 20th century have tended to suppose" 
4. You say that choosing between materialist monism and consciousness monism isn't the point, but it is. 
Consciousness monism or "mind first" must show how mind turns into matter. Materialistic monism or "matter first" 
must do the opposite, demonstrating how matter can produce mind. Even though the "matter first" crowd are far ahead 
in scientific acceptance, in fact we see mind produce matter every time a synaptic gap is crossed by neurotransmitters or 
hormones are triggered by experience. A person who is phobic sees a spider and reacts violently at the level of stress 
hormones, elevated heart rate, elevated blood pressure, etc. This physical state is entirely produced by a mental 
interpretation that a harmless insect is cause for panic.
