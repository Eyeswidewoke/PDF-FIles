Findings for 2008-Plea-Deal-Analysis.md

FINDING: The 2007–2008 Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA) between federal prosecutors and Jeffrey Epstein granted Epstein an extraordinarily lenient plea deal and immunized all his co-conspirators from federal charges. Epstein signed the NPA on September 24, 2007 (finalized in 2008) as part of a plea to two state prostitution charges. In exchange, **the U.S. Attorney’s Office (led by Alexander Acosta) agreed not to prosecute Epstein or “any potential co-conspirators”, explicitly naming four of his female associates: Sarah Kellen, Adriana Ross, Lesley Groff, and Nadia Marcinkova. The exact language of the co-conspirator clause reads: “the United States also agrees that it will not institute any criminal charges against any potential co-conspirators of Epstein, including but not limited to Sarah Kellen, Adriana Ross, Lesley Groff, or Nadia Marcinkova.” This effectively shut down the ongoing federal sex-trafficking investigation in Florida once Epstein pleaded guilty to the lesser state charges. Acosta and Epstein’s attorneys (including Jay Lefkowitz) negotiated the NPA in secret, and victims were not informed – a violation of the Crime Victims’ Rights Act that later prompted a lawsuit. Acosta’s stated rationale for the deal (when it became public in 2019) was that securing some jail time and restitution via a state plea was better than risking no conviction at all; he also claimed the case was complex and that the state charges ensured Epstein would register as a sex offender. A 2020 Department of Justice review by the Office of Professional Responsibility found Acosta exercised “poor judgment” in approving such a deal, though it stopped short of finding misconduct or illicit influence – noting that Acosta’s decision fell within prosecutorial discretion and was “not the result of improper factors.” In sum, the NPA allowed Epstein to serve just 13 months in a county jail (with liberal work release) and protected any accomplices from federal charges, a highly unusual outcome for a case involving dozens of underage victims.

United States (S.D. Fla. 2019) confirming the NPA’s terms and that victims were kept in the dark.

 

DATE: Agreement negotiated summer 2007, signed September 24, 2007, and Epstein pleaded guilty in June 2008 (state court). The deal became public in 2008–2009 when challenged by victims; DOJ’s internal review report was released November 2020.

 

VERIFICATION: Verified by primary legal documents. The NPA’s co-conspirator immunity clause is quoted directly from the agreement. Acosta’s involvement and reasoning are documented in his July 2019 press conference (when he was Labor Secretary) and the DOJ OPR findings. The OPR report (obtained by AP) explicitly states Acosta’s deal was “a flawed mechanism” that “constituted poor judgment,” but it found no evidence that Acosta was bribed or influenced by higher-ups. This aligns with court records indicating Acosta believed the plea was the “best” outcome under the circumstances, however controversial. Multiple independent sources (Miami Herald investigations, court transcripts) corroborate the sequence of events and the secrecy surrounding the NPA.

 

GAPS: Why such a lenient deal was struck remains somewhat murky. Acosta claimed in 2019 that “times were different” and that the focus was ensuring Epstein “would be punished” at all, but many find that unsatisfactory given the trove of evidence. Internal DOJ emails from 2007–08 (released in litigation) show disagreement between FBI agents and prosecutors about the deal, but much of the behind-closed-doors rationale is redacted or not documented. Notably, the “co-conspirator” immunity was unusually broad – it had no expiration and no geographic limit, potentially shielding Epstein’s helpers everywhere. It’s unclear who exactly requested that clause; Acosta has implied Epstein’s lawyers pushed hard for it. There is no paper trail of higher DOJ officials ordering Acosta to stand down – the 2020 IG/OPR investigation found no evidence of political pressure. Thus, we lack a full explanation for why DOJ acceded to terms that so favored Epstein. Additionally, while the NPA mentions that it could be made void if Epstein violated terms, it’s unknown if DOJ seriously revisited charges when Epstein was arrested again in 2019, or if the prior immunity for co-conspirators complicated new prosecutions (Maxwell’s 2021 case was brought in New York, technically outside the Florida agreement’s direct scope).

 

CONTRADICTIONS: A persistent claim in Epstein lore is that Alexander Acosta was told to back off because Epstein “belonged to intelligence.” This stems from a 2019 Daily Beast report by Vicky Ward, in which an anonymous source alleged that during Acosta’s vetting for Trump’s cabinet, he said he was told Epstein “belonged to intelligence” and to leave the case alone. However, there is no official confirmation of this claim. In fact, Acosta flatly denied it under oath. In a 2019 congressional hearing and to DOJ investigators, Acosta stated “The answer is no” when asked if he knew of any intelligence community ties to Epstein. He further told Congress in 2023 that he “would hesitate to take this reporting as fact.” In other words, the “Epstein was an intelligence asset” theory remains unproven, supported only by an unsourced anecdote. All named officials in the DOJ’s Epstein plea deal review similarly said they saw no evidence of Epstein being protected by intelligence agencies. This directly contradicts the conspiracy narrative. The contrast between Ward’s hearsay and Acosta’s sworn testimony is stark: if Acosta’s denial is truthful, then the lenient plea was more likely due to prosecutorial misjudgment (as the OPR report found) rather than a CIA/Mossad scheme. The intelligence rumor has nevertheless taken hold in popular imagination, illustrating how the secrecy of the NPA and Epstein’s unexplained wealth have created fertile ground for speculation.

Findings for Epstein-Files-Transparency-Act.md

FINDING: The Epstein Files Transparency Act is a new federal law (Public Law 119-38) enacted in November 2025 requiring the DOJ to disclose all unclassified records related to Jeffrey Epstein’s crimes and associates. It was introduced as H.R. 4405 (119th Congress) by Rep. Ro Khanna (D-CA), with bipartisan support including Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY) as a leading co-sponsor. The bill sailed through Congress – the House passed it 427–1 on November 18, 2025, and the Senate unanimously passed it the next day. President Trump signed it into law on November 19, 2025. The Act’s requirements are explicit: within 30 days of enactment, the Attorney General “shall… make publicly available, in a searchable downloadable format, all unclassified records, documents, communications, and investigative materials” in DOJ’s possession related to the investigation and prosecution of Jeffrey Epstein. This sweeping mandate includes records about Epstein’s co-conspirators (e.g. Ghislaine Maxwell), flight logs and travel records, and the names of any individuals (including government officials) referenced in connection with Epstein’s case. The Act does allow some narrow exceptions: DOJ may withhold (or redact) personal identifying information of victims or information that would jeopardize an active investigation. But importantly, the law forbids withholding or redacting files to protect people from embarrassment or to shield “politically exposed persons.” It also required, within 15 days after the release, a detailed report to Congress enumerating all categories of material released vs. withheld, a summary of redactions, and crucially “a list of all government officials and politically exposed persons” named in the files. In essence, the law sought to force full transparency about Epstein’s network and any potential high-level complicity, with a very aggressive timeline (by Dec 19, 2025 everything should have been out).

Katie Britt’s press release on Senate passage, and joint letters by Khanna/Massie urging enforcement).

 

DATE: Introduced July 15, 2025; Passed House Nov 18, 2025; Passed Senate & Signed into law Nov 19, 2025. Statutory deadline for full release: Dec 19, 2025 (30 days post-enactment).

 

VERIFICATION: The Act’s provisions are confirmed by the statutory text and CRS summary on Congress’s official site. The overwhelming vote counts are recorded in the House Clerk’s records and Senate journal. Contemporary news coverage (AP, NPR) noted that the bill was passed in reaction to public outcry for Epstein case transparency and had near-universal support. There is no ambiguity in what the law commands: it is one of the most sweeping declassification mandates ever passed for a criminal case. For verification, one can see the law included in the U.S. Statutes at Large (probably as 139 Stat. 657) – it’s a binding legal requirement on DOJ. The inclusion of Maxwell, flight logs, etc. is explicitly listed in the bill’s text, so there’s no doubt about Congress’s intent.

 

GAPS: DOJ missed the deadline. By law, everything unclassified should have been public by 12/19/2025, but that did not happen. DOJ released a batch on Dec 19 (roughly 125k pages) and claimed the rest would come “on a rolling basis.” Indeed, as of mid-January 2026 – nearly a month after the deadline – over 2 million documents were still unreleased. DOJ lawyers, in a Jan 5, 2026 court filing, admitted they had posted only 12,285 documents (~125,575 pages), i.e. “less than 1%” of the total Epstein file archive. They also revealed an ever-growing universe of potentially responsive material (over 2 million documents identified, amounting to “more than 300 gigabytes of data and physical evidence” in FBI’s case system). This raises a gap between the law’s requirements and DOJ’s compliance. The law set a legal obligation, not a suggestion, yet DOJ has not met it. Congress expected a comprehensive list of names of officials in the files by Jan 2026, but that report is presumably delayed or incomplete (as of the Guardian’s reporting on Jan 19, 2026, DOJ had also “flouted” the requirement to report to the Judiciary Committees). What percentage of the files have actually been released? As of early January, literally <1% by document count (12k out of 2+ million). After DOJ’s second release on Jan 30, 2026 of about 3 million pages, the total might be around 3.1 million pages out of an estimated 5–6 million pages universe (since DOJ said 6 million docs including duplicates). If 300 GB was the estimate, perhaps ~4-5% of data was released by end of Jan 2026. Clearly, full compliance is far off, leaving a gap that may need further congressional or court action. Another gap: We don’t know if DOJ will release the “list of all government officials” mentioned in the Act. That could be politically explosive (naming every Congressperson, diplomat, etc. found in the files). By law they must, but given the delay, that aspect is pending.

 

CONTRADICTIONS: President Trump and his DOJ touted the Act as a commitment to transparency, yet DOJ’s slow-roll implementation seems to contradict the law’s spirit and letter. The Act passed with near unanimity, suggesting a rare bipartisan agreement for full disclosure; however, DOJ under Trump missed the deadline and invoked various hurdles (privacy, volume, ongoing reviews) – which contradicts the clear deadline Congress set. In fact, Reps. Khanna and Massie wrote to a judge that “DOJ failed to meet the Act’s requirements in multiple respects, including missing the deadline, asserting privileges the Act does not permit, and over-redacting despite the Act’s prohibitions.” They even suggested a special master oversee DOJ. So we have the Executive branch seemingly undermining a law signed by that very Executive. Another contradiction is in DOJ’s messaging: On one hand, Bondi/Trump claimed credit for “releasing Epstein files” in Feb 2025, but on the other hand they withheld the bulk of documents when legally mandated to release them, citing the need to protect victims. The nearly unified congressional voice (<1% dissent on the vote) underscores how strong the expectation was for openness, which contrasts with DOJ’s cautious approach. Additionally, when DOJ says it found no “incriminating” evidence of certain things (client list, etc.) and then heavily redacts and delays, it feeds a public perception that the government might be hiding something – essentially contradicting the Act’s purpose of boosting public trust. We also see contradictory pressures: the Act says political figures can’t be protected, yet the very slow pace and the Trump-flagging incident suggests political sensitivity was a factor, at least initially. In conclusion, the Epstein Files Transparency Act set a clear, demanding standard for DOJ, and DOJ’s actual performance has been at odds with that – a point not lost on lawmakers or the public.

Findings for Inner-Circle-Co-Conspirators.md

FINDING: Epstein’s inner circle of alleged co-conspirators – notably Sarah Kellen, Nadia Marcinkova, Lesley Groff, and Adriana Ross – were never charged, largely due to the 2007 NPA’s immunity clause and prosecutorial decisions. They were, according to victims and investigators, integral to Epstein’s operation: Kellen (Epstein’s scheduler) and Groff (his executive assistant) managed logistics of the abuse, often booking “massage” appointments with underage girls; Marcinkova (sometimes spelled “Nada Marcinkova”) and Ross were also aides who allegedly participated in or facilitated sexual encounters. After Epstein’s 2019 re-arrest, the U.S. Attorney’s Office in New York (SDNY) publicly stated it was not bound by the old Florida NPA and that those four could still be prosecuted despite their immunity deal. This gave hope that they might finally be brought to justice. However, in practice, none of the four were ever indicted in the SDNY case or elsewhere. Ghislaine Maxwell was the only co-conspirator charged and convicted (in 2021) by federal prosecutors. During Maxwell’s trial, these women featured prominently: for instance, victim Carolyn testified that Sarah Kellen not only scheduled her visits to Epstein but also paid her $500 after sexualized massages and even took nude photos of her when Carolyn was 14–15. Kellen’s name came up more than 80 times at trial, underscoring her role. Despite this, Kellen was not called as a witness by either side – a sign that she likely was not cooperating with the government. Kellen, now in her 40s and married (to a racecar driver, taking his surname), has publicly claimed through lawyers that she herself was a victim brainwashed by Epstein, essentially casting herself as another pawn rather than a perpetrator. Lesley Groff, Epstein’s long-time New York secretary, similarly maintained innocence and even got favorable signals: in December 2021, her attorneys announced that after a two-year federal investigation, the DOJ informed them “no criminal charges will be brought against Lesley Groff.” Groff had been sued in New York by one victim (Jennifer Araoz) for enabling Epstein; that civil case was dropped (reportedly as part of a settlement) in late 2021. Adriana Ross (a former Epstein assistant from Eastern Europe) and Nadia Marcinkova (who was often described as Epstein’s onetime “girlfriend” who also procured girls) have kept very low profiles. Marcinkova, whom Epstein reportedly once referred to as his “Yugoslavian sex slave” and who was a teenager living with Epstein in the early 2000s, was also protected by the NPA. Post-2019, it does not appear she was ever subpoenaed or pressed to testify – at least not publicly – and she has not faced charges. Ross likewise vanished from public view. In short, these four women – key facilitators by many accounts – have faced no legal consequences (unlike Maxwell). The likely reason is that the immunity clause tied prosecutors’ hands for the Florida offenses, and by the time of the 2019 NY case, prosecutors chose to focus on Maxwell as the primary co-conspirator. It’s possible they assessed that the others’ conduct, while reprehensible, either overlapped with Maxwell’s (making additional charges duplicative) or lacked sufficient new evidence beyond what was known in 2007. Another factor: some of these women may have quietly cooperated to some extent. There were rumors in 2019 that at least one of Epstein’s former assistants provided helpful info to investigators – though who and what was never confirmed.

FINDING: Epstein’s Flight Logs – One of the most significant sets of evidence released are the flight manifests for Epstein’s private planes, which detail the prominent passengers who flew with him. Complete list of what’s been released vs withheld: As of now, the 1997–2008 logs (basically the period covering Epstein’s heyday until his first incarceration) have been released and reported on. The later logs (2009–2019) are less complete publicly; however, Epstein did less high-profile ferrying in that era due to being a registered sex offender and losing his main jet. In summary, the flight manifests are accessible (compiled by journalists at sites like Insider, and now part of DOJ’s “Epstein files” website), and they provide a key inventory of Epstein’s travel companions. They have been crucial in verifying claims, e.g., Giuffre’s assertion that Trump flew on Epstein’s plane was bolstered by the logs showing a 1990s flight with Trump, and Clinton’s extensive travels are clearly recorded.

FINDING: Epstein and Brunel’s Custodial Deaths – Both Jeffrey Epstein (2019) and his close associate Jean-Luc Brunel (2022) died in jail under questionable circumstances officially ruled as suicides. Epstein’s death, on August 10, 2019 at Manhattan’s Metropolitan Correctional Center, was investigated by the FBI and DOJ Inspector General. The DOJ claimed the video was “full raw footage,” which was false; the metadata suggests it was altered, though reasons are unknown (could be a technical glitch or something more nefarious). All this fuels speculation that Epstein’s death was not a simple suicide. Notably, Dr. Michael Baden, a forensic pathologist hired by Epstein’s brother, observed Epstein’s autopsy and stated in 2019 that the neck injuries (a broken hyoid bone, etc.) were more consistent with homicidal strangulation than hanging. Reports say there were no cameras in the cell and like Epstein, he apparently used bedsheets. Brunel’s death meant he never faced trial; French prosecutors had been building a case with multiple women accusing him of rape or trafficking. Statistically, two key figures in the same sex-trafficking ring dying by hanging in custody is extraordinary. Is there documentation of the “intelligence” claim about Epstein? – There is no official document in which Alexander Acosta or any U.S. official says “Epstein belonged to intelligence.” The entire claim traces back to a single second-hand source in a July 2019 Daily Beast article by Vicky Ward. What are the exact terms of the NPA co-conspirator clause? – The Non-Prosecution Agreement (NPA) signed on Sept 24, 2007 contains a paragraph 10 that states in full: “[I]f Epstein successfully fulfills all the terms and conditions of this agreement, the United States also agrees that it will not institute any criminal charges against any potential co-conspirators of Epstein, including but not limited to Sarah Kellen, Adriana Ross, Lesley Groff, or Nadia Marcinkova.”. This is the co-conspirator immunity clause. Key points: it has no expiration date or geographical limit (it doesn’t say “in the Southern District of Florida” – it was interpreted as nationwide immunity for those individuals for offenses up to 2007). It also says “including but not limited to” those four names, implying others not named could be considered covered if they were potential co-conspirators. The clause was extraordinarily broad – essentially unheard of in plea deals. To break it down: Epstein’s end of the deal was to plead guilty to two state charges, register as a sex offender, pay restitution, etc., and in exchange not only would he avoid federal charges, but all his accomplices would too. The clause specifically names the four women who were known to have been heavily involved (Kellen, Ross, Groff, Marcinkova), making clear they’re immunized. So, the exact language: “…will not institute any criminal charges against any potential co-conspirators of Epstein, including but not limited to [the four names].”. Source: the NPA document (see above citation) – this is a direct quote.

Why weren’t facilitators (Kellen, Marcinkova, etc.) charged? – As discussed in the inner circle findings, the primary reason is the 2007 NPA granted them immunity from federal prosecution – at least in Florida. While SDNY in New York argued it wasn’t bound by that deal, in practice those women were part of the same conspiracy timeframe as Epstein/Maxwell, and prosecutors may have felt the NPA’s existence (and due process fairness) made charging them difficult. Additionally, by 2019-2020, the evidence against them was old and largely testimonial; Maxwell was clearly the bigger target to hold accountable publicly. DOJ may also have reasoned that charging these subordinates might complicate the case or that some of them cooperated behind closed doors. For instance, Sarah Kellen and Lesley Groff both were reportedly subpoenaed to the federal grand jury in 2020 – neither testified at Maxwell’s trial, suggesting they invoked 5th Amendment. Instead of charging them, DOJ possibly left the door open for their cooperation, but apparently didn’t get any that was worth a cooperation deal. There’s also speculation that optics and resources played a role: going after four mid-level women who could claim “we were under Epstein/Maxwell’s influence” might have been a messy, not guaranteed case, especially after Maxwell’s conviction satisfied the public demand for accountability to a degree. It’s also possible DOJ felt that since these women were named (and shamed) in public documents and some faced civil suits, that was a sufficient outcome given evidentiary challenges. Officially, when Groff’s attorneys announced no charges, they indicated DOJ had done a “thorough review” and decided not to prosecute. This implies lack of new evidence to overcome any immunity or reasonable-doubt issues. In summary: They weren’t charged because (a) the NPA shielded them (at least in their view), (b) DOJ prioritized Maxwell as the big fish, (c) evidentiary constraints and passage of time, and (d) possibly prosecutorial discretion that further trials wouldn’t significantly add to justice achieved. Source: NPA text immunizing them; Guardian noting SDNY said could charge them but didn’t; Groff attorney statement about DOJ no-charge.

What did the 2011 Epstein email about Trump actually say in full? – In November 2025, House Oversight Democrats released the full text of an email Jeffrey Epstein sent to Ghislaine Maxwell on January 9, 2011. According to that release, the email reads: “I want you to realize that that **dog that hasn’t barked is Trump.. [Victim’s name] spent hours at my house with him,, he has never once been mentioned. Police chief. etc. I’m 75% there.”. In context, Epstein was telling Maxwell that Trump – who was then a prominent businessman (and would later be President) – had been involved (spent hours with a known underage victim at Epstein’s home) yet notably had not been implicated or “barked” in any investigation. “Dog that hasn’t barked” is a phrase meaning something conspicuously not attracting attention. The email suggests Epstein was somewhat baffled or relieved that Trump’s name had not come up with law enforcement (“Police chief, etc.”). Maxwell replies: “I have been thinking about that…”, indicating she too finds it noteworthy. So the full email exchange (Epstein to Maxwell, Maxwell’s short reply) shows Epstein referencing a specific victim who allegedly interacted with Trump at Epstein’s house while underage, and remarking that Trump was never investigated for it. This email was previously unseen until the Oversight Committee released it. It corroborates what Giuffre said in depositions (that Trump was a friend of Epstein but she herself did not claim he abused her; nonetheless another victim apparently did work at Mar-a-Lago and met Trump while underage via Epstein – possibly referring to someone like Maria Farmer or another minor working events). Importantly, Trump “never once been mentioned” suggests to Epstein that Trump’s involvement was kept quiet. This email is explosive because it’s Epstein’s own words hinting at Trump’s knowledge or presence. In another email (2015) Epstein explicitly told Michael Wolff “Of course he [Trump] knew about the girls as he asked Ghislaine to stop” recruiting at Mar-a-Lago. But focusing on the 2011 one: it basically says: Trump spent hours with an underage girl at Epstein’s house, but his name never came up in police inquiries – a silent dog. That is the plain reading. Source: House Oversight Dems press release (Nov 12, 2025) containing the emails verbatim.

Has Maxwell formally offered to testify? – Yes. In July 2025, after being subpoenaed by the House Oversight Committee, Ghislaine Maxwell (through her attorney) sent a letter offering to testify if granted full immunity or clemency. The letter to Chairman James Comer said Maxwell would “cooperate with Congress if a fair and safe path forward can be established,” then listed conditions: a “formal grant of immunity” from prosecution, that she not testify from prison (due to safety), that she receive the questions in advance, and that the deposition occur after her pending appeals are resolved. Essentially, Maxwell was demanding either immunity or even suggesting she’d be “willing and eager” to testify in exchange for a presidential clemency (pardon/commutation). She even floated that if she got clemency, she’d testify openly in Congress. So she has offered, but with strings attached that are highly unlikely to be met. As expected, the Oversight Committee replied that they would not consider granting immunity. And obviously, clemency is in the hands of the President (and politically radioactive). So while Maxwell has put an offer on the table, it’s seen as a desperate bid – she’s currently serving 20 years. She hasn’t indicated willingness to talk without those conditions. In summary: Maxwell signaled she’d tell what she “knows” if she gets out of prison or at least is legally protected, but otherwise she invoked the Fifth Amendment. And indeed, she has not testified to Congress as of now because her conditions were not met. Source: Guardian article on Maxwell’s letter and Committee response.

What’s the current status of ongoing investigations? – As of early 2026:

U.S. Investigations: The DOJ’s criminal investigation effectively ended with Maxwell’s conviction (2021) and the closing of ancillary probes. No further federal charges are pending against others (DOJ stated in 2022 that the investigation “continues” in a sense of reviewing evidence for the Epstein Files Act, but no new targets have been announced). The focus shifted to compliance with the Transparency Act. In Congress, House Oversight’s investigation (Republican-led) is ongoing into DOJ’s handling of Epstein files, but that’s oversight, not a criminal probe. They’ve held hearings (Bondi testified in late 2025) and may issue reports. State-level: The U.S. Virgin Islands pursued civil litigation (not criminal) against Epstein’s estate and accomplices – those cases concluded in late 2022/2023 with settlements (e.g., JPMorgan paying $290M to victims, Epstein estate paying over $105M to USVI) and revealed some evidence of how Epstein moved money through banks. The USVI Attorney General’s investigation is essentially over after those settlements, though USVI did say it would keep an eye out if new info emerges. So no ongoing active criminal investigation in USVI either.

Prince Andrew (UK): He faced a civil lawsuit by Virginia Giuffre, which he settled in February 2022 reportedly for around £12 million (no admission of liability). As for criminal, the UK’s Metropolitan Police had opened and closed an investigation multiple times. In October 2019, Met Police said they wouldn’t take further action regarding Epstein’s UK activities. After Maxwell’s conviction, the Met reviewed materials again but in January 2022 they confirmed no charges for Prince Andrew or others in UK due to either lack of evidence or that the incidents occurred outside UK jurisdiction. So currently, there is no active criminal investigation of Prince Andrew in the UK. He’s essentially a free man legally, albeit having stepped back from public life.

France: After Brunel’s arrest (2020) and suicide (2022), the French investigation into Epstein’s French connections largely stalled. They had identified some victims in France and were looking at Brunel and possibly others. With Brunel dead, French authorities haven’t charged anyone else. In 2019, French police did seize some evidence from Epstein’s Paris apartment. But as of 2023, France has not announced further prosecutions. So effectively closed, unless new victims press charges independently. Some French victims are pursuing civil suits.

Other jurisdictions: There was talk of perhaps investigating Epstein’s activities in other places (e.g., Brazil, due to a model agency link, or Israel via conspiracy theories), but nothing concrete is known.

Financial investigations: One aspect is ongoing scrutiny of how Epstein made his money. The U.S. Virgin Islands vs JPMorgan case unearthed a lot of records (internal emails, deposition of Jes Staley) showing how banks overlooked Epstein’s suspicious transactions. That case settled in late 2023, but JPMorgan agreed to implement anti-trafficking measures. No individual bankers were criminally charged; however, the DOJ did reportedly open an investigation into JPMorgan’s compliance failures (unclear status). In short, financial probes seem to have ended with settlements, not criminal charges.

In summary, active investigations have mostly concluded: Maxwell is convicted and appealing (her appeal to the 2nd Circuit was denied in 2023; she may try the Supreme Court, slim chance). Congress is still investigating DOJ/FBI’s handling but that’s political. So we are in the phase of information release and hindsight, rather than ongoing law enforcement action.
